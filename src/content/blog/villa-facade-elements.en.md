A classical villa facade consists of a coordinated set of stone elements, each with structural and decorative roles. Understanding these components is essential for architects, clients, and interior designers — in new design or renovation. This guide explains main villa facade stone elements with examples from [Eskan Stone](/en/projects) projects.

## Main Classical Facade Components

### Capital

The capital sits atop the column, marking the transition from shaft to entablature or cornice. In classical orders, three main types exist: Doric (plain), Ionic (volutes), and Corinthian (acanthus leaves).

In contemporary Iranian projects, **composite capitals** — combining stone and sometimes fiber — are common. CNC production enables exact pattern repetition.

### Cornice

The cornice defines the building's horizontal line and sheds rain from the wall. Multi-layer cornices (bed mould, corona, cyma) add shadow depth and facade dimension.

**Technical note:** Outer cornice slope should be at least 3 degrees outward to prevent standing water.

### Stone Wall Panel

Panels cover the main facade surface. In classical facades, panels are separated by **horizontal and vertical joints** to modulate building scale.

- Typical thickness: 30–40 mm
- Installation: mechanical anchors or structural adhesive

### Quoin

Reinforcing stones at building corners with decorative and structural roles. In classical architecture, quoins are larger and more prominent than intermediate panels.

### Column and Pilaster

A **column** is a load-bearing vertical element; a **pilaster** is a decorative column attached to the wall. Both create vertical rhythm on luxury villa facades.

## Visual Composition Principles

Classical facade design principles:

1. **Symmetry** on the main entrance axis
2. **Hierarchy** — entrance more prominent than windows
3. **Proportion** — floor heights relative to column widths
4. **Texture** — matte and polished contrast between panel and cornice

## Common Facade Design Mistakes

- Cornice without adequate drip slope
- Panel and cornice thickness mismatch
- Decorative columns without proper stone base
- Ignoring thermal expansion in joints
